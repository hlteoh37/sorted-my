👶 Registering a Newborn in Malaysia (Birth Certificate)

Hospital counter, JPN, documents, naming rules, MyKid, and what happens if you miss the 14-day deadline.

Deadline
14 days (hospital) / 42 days (JPN)
Fee (On Time)
Free
Fee (Late)
RM5–RM100+
Key Body
JPN / Hospital Counter
Easiest route: Most government hospitals in Malaysia have a JPN registration counter on-site. You can register your baby before you even leave the hospital — usually within 1–2 days of birth. This is the fastest and most stress-free option.

Understanding the Deadlines

Important: Registering late is not just a financial penalty — it can create complications with the child's nationality status, school enrollment, and eligibility for government benefits. Register as early as possible.

Method 1: Hospital JPN Counter (Recommended)

Most government hospitals (hospital kerajaan) have a JPN registration counter inside the maternity ward or hospital administration block. Private hospitals may not have this — check with your hospital before delivery.

  1. Ask hospital staff about the JPN counter location — usually on the maternity ward floor or ground floor. A nurse or midwife will typically advise you before discharge.
  2. Bring required documents — see checklist below. Both parents should ideally be present, or the father can bring a letter of consent if the mother is still in the ward.
  3. Submit the registration form (Borang JPN.LM01) — completed by the hospital and countersigned by the parents. The JPN officer verifies and processes it on-site.
  4. Receive the birth certificate (Sijil Kelahiran) — issued immediately or within 1 working day. The certificate is the official document you will use for MyKid, school registration, and all future government dealings.

Method 2: JPN Office Walk-In

If you gave birth at a private hospital without a JPN counter, or missed the hospital registration window, go to any JPN office within 42 days.

  1. Bring all required documents (see checklist below).
  2. Take a queue number at the JPN office. Bring both parents if possible — one parent can register, but having both avoids complications with citizenship determination.
  3. Submit Borang JPN.LM01 — the counter officer will assist you in completing the form if needed.
  4. Birth certificate is issued on the spot or within 1 working day.
Note: You can register at any JPN office in Malaysia — you do not need to go to the JPN in your home state or the state where the birth occurred.

Documents Checklist

For Married Couples (Both Malaysian Citizens)

  • Hospital discharge document / birth notification — issued by the hospital (Borang Hospital or notification slip)
  • Father's MyKad — original and 1 photocopy
  • Mother's MyKad — original and 1 photocopy
  • Marriage certificate — original and 1 photocopy (must be registered under JPN or State Religious Department)
  • Borang JPN.LM01 — registration form (provided at the hospital or JPN counter)

For Unmarried Mothers or Single Parents

  • Hospital discharge document
  • Mother's MyKad — original and 1 photocopy
  • Statutory declaration (if applicable) — for acknowledgement of paternity by unmarried father
  • Note: The child's surname and birth certificate will reflect the mother's family name if parents are unmarried, unless the father formally acknowledges paternity

For Foreign Spouses

  • Foreign parent's passport — original and 1 photocopy
  • Malaysian parent's MyKad — original and 1 photocopy
  • Marriage certificate — if registered overseas, a certified translation may be required
  • Long-term pass / MyPR / PR documents — if applicable

Naming Rules in Malaysia

Malaysia's naming conventions vary by ethnicity and religion. JPN follows specific rules for each group.

  • Malay names (Muslim) — typically use "bin" (son of) or "binti" (daughter of) followed by the father's name. Example: Ahmad bin Abdullah. The child does not take a family surname.
  • Chinese names — typically use the father's surname (family name) as the child's last name. Both Chinese characters and romanised spelling are registered.
  • Indian names — typically use "a/l" (anak lelaki / son of) or "a/p" (anak perempuan / daughter of) followed by the father's name. Example: Rajan a/l Krishnan.
  • Prohibited names: Numbers, symbols, offensive words, titles (Dato', Tan Sri, etc.) are not permitted as given names.
  • Name changes after registration are possible but require a separate application (Permohonan Pertukaran Nama) and additional fees. Change within 1 year is RM10; after 1 year is more complex.

After Registration: Next Steps

MySejahtera Health Book (Buku Kesihatan Kanak-Kanak)

The hospital issues a physical health book (buku merah / buku kesihatan) for your baby at discharge. Keep this book — it records all vaccinations and health checks throughout childhood. You will need it at every clinic visit and is required for school enrollment.

Applying for MyKid

  1. MyKid is the official identity card for children aged 0–11. It is optional but strongly recommended — it simplifies all future dealings with government agencies.
  2. Apply at any JPN office after the birth certificate is issued. Bring: birth certificate (original), both parents' MyKad, and RM10 application fee.
  3. The child's photo and thumb/finger impression are taken at the JPN counter.
  4. MyKid is issued within 1 working day at most JPN offices.

Registering for Tax Relief

  • Child tax relief — parents can claim RM2,000 per child (under 18) in annual income tax returns
  • This is automatic once the birth certificate is registered under JPN — declare in your e-Filing on the LHDN website

Late Registration

If you miss the 42-day window, you can still register but additional steps are required.

  1. Go to JPN and explain the situation. Bring all documents including the hospital discharge slip.
  2. Pay the late penalty fee — RM5 to RM50 for registrations between 43 days and 1 year. Fees increase with delay.
  3. For registrations over 1 year old: You must submit a Statutory Declaration (Akuan Bersumpah), sworn before a Commissioner of Oaths, explaining the delay. JPN reviews these cases individually — extra documentation (e.g. hospital records, witness statements) may be required.
  4. Court order (for very late cases): In extreme cases (years of delay), a court order may be required before JPN can issue the birth certificate. Consult a lawyer if you are in this situation.
Do not delay. An unregistered child has no legal identity in Malaysia. They cannot attend school, obtain government assistance, or obtain a passport. Register as soon as possible, no matter how late you already are.

Twins and Multiple Births

  • Each child must be registered separately — one registration form (Borang JPN.LM01) per child
  • All are free of charge if registered within the 42-day window
  • Birth order (first born, second born) is recorded on the certificate if disclosed
  • At hospital counters, the staff usually process multiple registrations simultaneously for twins — just inform them at the start
