What is JPJ e-Tukar?

JPJ e-Tukar (also written "eTukar") is the Jabatan Pengangkutan Jalan (JPJ) online vehicle ownership transfer system. It allows both private individuals and dealers to process vehicle ownership transfers digitally — reducing the need for multiple counter visits and speeding up the process.

Before e-Tukar, both buyer and seller had to queue at JPJ counters with a stack of physical forms. Now, the application can be initiated online via the MyJPJ app or the JPJ portal, with counter attendance required only for identity verification and document submission.

Who must complete the transfer? Malaysian law requires ownership to be transferred within 7 days of the vehicle changing hands. Failure to do so means the registered owner (seller) remains legally liable for the vehicle — including accidents, fines, and road tax.

The e-Tukar process covers all private vehicle transfers (motorcycles and cars) between individuals in Malaysia. It does not apply to commercial vehicles, which use a separate process.

Documents Needed

From the Seller

  • MyKad (original) — seller must be present or grant Power of Attorney to a registered runner.
  • Vehicle registration card / Grant (Geran / V5) — the original ownership document. If the vehicle has an outstanding hire purchase, the bank holds this; you need a bank redemption letter first.
  • Hire purchase settlement letter (if applicable) — issued by the bank confirming the loan is fully settled and the grant has been released.

From the Buyer

  • MyKad (original) — buyer must be present or grant Power of Attorney to a registered runner.
  • Insurance cover note / Certificate of Insurance — valid insurance in the buyer's name is required before JPJ will process the transfer.
  • JPJ K3 form — the official vehicle ownership transfer application form. Available at JPJ counters or downloadable from the JPJ website.

Vehicle Documents

  • PUSPAKOM B2 inspection report — confirms roadworthiness and that the vehicle chassis/engine numbers match the grant.
  • Road tax disc — should be present on the windscreen. If expired, it will need to be renewed as part of the transfer process.
Lost grant? If the grant is lost, the seller must apply for a replacement at JPJ before the transfer can proceed. This adds time and cost (approximately RM50–100). A "lost" grant can also indicate the car is still under a bank's possession — verify carefully.

Step-by-Step Process

  1. Settle any outstanding hire purchase

    If the vehicle has an active bank loan, the bank holds the original grant. The seller must obtain a redemption letter from the bank and fully settle the outstanding loan amount. Only after full settlement will the bank release the original grant (V5) to allow the transfer.

  2. Check and clear outstanding summons

    Use the MyJPJ app or check at any JPJ counter to see if the vehicle has any outstanding traffic or road tax summons. All summons must be paid before the transfer can proceed. You can use this to negotiate a price reduction with the seller — agree who pays the outstanding summons.

  3. PUSPAKOM B2 inspection

    Bring the vehicle to any PUSPAKOM (Pusat Pemeriksaan Kenderaan Berkomputer) centre for a B2 roadworthiness inspection. Walk-ins are accepted at most centres. The inspection typically takes 30–60 minutes and costs RM50–150 depending on vehicle size. PUSPAKOM will issue an inspection report — keep this safe as you need it for JPJ.

  4. Buyer obtains insurance cover note

    Before visiting JPJ, the buyer must have valid motor insurance in their own name. Contact an insurance agent or insurer (e.g., Allianz, Etiqa, Zurich) to get a cover note. The cover note is usually issued same-day. Note: you cannot renew road tax without valid insurance.

  5. Complete the JPJ K3 form

    Download the K3 form from the JPJ website or obtain one at the JPJ counter. Both buyer and seller fill in their details — full name, IC number, address, and vehicle details. Both parties sign the form.

  6. Visit JPJ counter together (or appoint a runner)

    Both buyer and seller must attend JPJ together, bringing all documents. Alternatively, both can authorise a licensed JPJ runner (kerani) via a Power of Attorney letter. The runner manages the entire submission on your behalf. Visit during weekday mornings for shorter queues — or use the MyJPJ app to book a queue number in advance.

  7. Submit documents and pay JPJ transfer fee

    Hand all documents to the JPJ officer: K3 form, both MyKads, original grant, PUSPAKOM report, insurance cover note. Pay the transfer fee of RM100 at the counter. JPJ will process the application and update the vehicle ownership record.

  8. Collect new grant (Geran)

    The updated grant showing the buyer's name as registered owner is typically ready within 1–3 working days. For same-day issuance, check if your JPJ branch offers express processing. The new grant is the buyer's most important proof of ownership — keep it safe.

  9. Renew road tax (if expired)

    If road tax is due or has lapsed, renew it immediately. Road tax can be renewed at JPJ, Pos Malaysia counters, or via MyEG/MyJPJ app. For vehicles over 5 years old, a PUSPAKOM B2 inspection (already done) is required for road tax renewal — so you can renew on the same visit.

One-stop kerani service: Licensed JPJ runners (kerani kereta) typically charge RM200–400 to handle PUSPAKOM, JPJ submission, insurance, and road tax as a bundled package. Useful if you want to avoid the queue and paperwork. Ensure they are JPJ-registered by asking for their JPJ agent number.

Common Pitfalls

Outstanding Summons Block the Transfer

JPJ will not process an ownership transfer if the vehicle has outstanding summons. Always check summons before meeting at JPJ — use the MyJPJ app or the PDRM Semak Saman portal. Negotiate with the seller to settle these, or deduct the summon amount from the purchase price.

Important: Summons follow the vehicle, not the owner. If you buy a car with unpaid summons, they become your problem — JPJ will hold future road tax renewals until cleared.

Hire Purchase Not Fully Settled

If the seller still has an active bank loan on the vehicle, the bank legally owns the car — the seller cannot legally transfer it. Always get confirmation directly from the bank (not just the seller's word) that the hire purchase is fully settled and the grant has been released.

Blacklisted Vehicles

A vehicle may be blacklisted by JPJ if it has been reported stolen, involved in fraud, or flagged for regulatory violations. A blacklisted vehicle cannot be transferred at all. Check using JPJ MyCarInfo before agreeing to purchase.

Mismatched Vehicle Details

PUSPAKOM checks that the chassis number and engine number match the grant. If there is a mismatch (possible sign of a recon/cloned vehicle), the transfer cannot proceed. Never buy a vehicle where numbers don't match the documents.

Expired or Missing Insurance

JPJ requires a valid insurance cover note in the buyer's name before processing the transfer. Don't show up at JPJ without it — you'll need to leave and come back. Arrange insurance the day before your JPJ appointment.

Both Parties Must Be Present (or a Runner)

JPJ requires both buyer and seller to attend, or both to appoint a licensed runner with notarised Power of Attorney letters. A seller who wants to send the grant by post and let the buyer handle everything is a red flag — this is not how the process works.

Pro Tips

  • Use the MyJPJ app to get a queue number before going. This saves 30–60 minutes compared to walk-in queuing at busy branches.
  • Go on a Tuesday or Wednesday morning. Monday and Friday afternoons tend to be the busiest at JPJ counters. Mid-week mornings are fastest.
  • Do PUSPAKOM before confirming the final sale price. If the vehicle fails inspection, you'll need the seller to fix the issues — factor this into price negotiations.
  • Ask your insurer for a multi-year cover note. Some insurers bundle a 1-year policy with the cover note at the point of transfer, saving you a separate trip later.
  • Keep the original grant in a safe place. The grant is your most important proof of ownership. Losing it means applying for a replacement at JPJ (costs time and RM50–100).
  • If using a kerani, verify their JPJ agent number. Unlicensed runners have operated scams — insist on seeing their authorisation before handing over any documents or payment.
  • Take photos of all documents before submission. Keep digital copies of your K3 form, PUSPAKOM report, and cover note in case anything is misplaced during the process.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I do the JPJ transfer without the seller being present?
Not directly. Both buyer and seller must be present at JPJ, or both must grant Power of Attorney to a licensed JPJ runner (kerani). The runner submits on behalf of both parties. A seller who mails you the grant and asks you to handle it yourself is not following correct procedure — and you'd have no legal protection if something goes wrong.
What is the difference between a B2 and a B5 PUSPAKOM inspection?
A B2 inspection is a roadworthiness check — it confirms the vehicle is safe to drive (lights, brakes, tyres, emissions, etc.) and is required for road tax renewal of vehicles over 5 years old. A B5 inspection is specifically for pre-ownership transfer verification — it confirms the vehicle's chassis and engine numbers match the grant. For a standard private vehicle transfer, a B2 inspection is typically required. Some JPJ branches may accept a combined B2 + B5. Check with your local JPJ or PUSPAKOM centre for current requirements.
How do I check if a car has outstanding summons before buying?
Use the MyJPJ app (for JPJ summons) and the PDRM Semak Saman portal (for PDRM/police summons) — enter the vehicle registration number. You can also check at any JPJ counter or PDRM station. Run this check before agreeing on a final price; any outstanding summons must be settled before the transfer can proceed.
The car I'm buying still has a hire purchase loan. What do I do?
The seller must settle the outstanding loan with their bank first. The bank will then issue a loan redemption letter and release the original grant (V5). Options: (1) the seller settles the loan before the sale, (2) you and the seller visit the bank together and you pay the outstanding amount on the seller's behalf (deduct from purchase price), or (3) use a kerani/agent who specialises in hire purchase redemption. Never pay the full purchase price before the loan is confirmed settled — the bank can repossess the car even after you've paid if the loan remains outstanding.
Can I drive the car home before the transfer is completed?
Legally, the car is still registered to the seller until the grant is updated. Driving it before the transfer creates ambiguity if you're stopped by police or get into an accident. In practice, many buyers drive briefly between PUSPAKOM and JPJ on the same day — but the risk is yours. Complete the transfer as quickly as possible, ideally within the same day or the following day.
What happens if I don't transfer ownership within 7 days?
The registered owner (seller) remains legally liable for the vehicle. If the vehicle is involved in an accident, the seller could face liability claims. Road tax and insurance complications also arise. JPJ can penalise late transfers, and the seller may apply to JPJ to report the vehicle as sold to protect themselves. Transfer as promptly as possible — there's no benefit to delaying.
Can a foreigner (non-Malaysian) register a vehicle in Malaysia?
Yes, foreigners with a valid Malaysian resident pass (MM2H, employment pass, etc.) can register and own a vehicle in Malaysia. They need their passport and relevant pass documents instead of a MyKad. The transfer process is otherwise the same. Short-term visitors cannot register vehicles in their name.
